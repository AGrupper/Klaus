## Core Principles Digest

### AM/PM Split — The Interference Mitigation Rule

Concurrent strength + endurance training competes at the molecular level: endurance
activates AMPK (energy-sensing pathway), which suppresses mTOR (the anabolic signaling
pathway driving muscle protein synthesis). This is the interference effect, and it is
real — but **entirely manageable with ≥6h session separation**. [PEER]

Amit's blueprint solves this correctly. AM run finishes by ~09:00. PM strength is ≥17:00.
Separation window: ≈8 hours — adequate for AMPK signal clearance and full anabolic
restoration before the lift. This is not aesthetic scheduling; it is the primary mitigation.

**The one red flag to volunteer immediately:** If Amit ever proposes heavy strength
immediately after a threshold run with no separation (e.g., same AM block), flag it.
AMPK will be elevated and the strength adaptation signal will be blunted.

Order rule for single-session training (rare for Amit — Sunday mixed practice): strength
before endurance is superior for lower-body adaptations. Sunday's mixed practice is
skill/capacity, not heavy strength, so order is less critical there.

### Session-by-Session Execution Cues

**Wednesday AM — Threshold Run (The 1:25 HM Engine):**
RPE 6–7 ("comfortably hard" — can say 3–4 words, not a sentence). Pace: 3:50–3:55/km
in blocks 1–3, locking to 4:01/km in Weeks 13–14. Volume builds 6km (Week 1) → 11km
(Week 11). Structure: 2–3km Zone-2 warm-up → threshold intervals → 1–2km cool-down.
Critical discipline: **do not exceed target pace on good days** — pace consistency over

**Monday + Thursday AM — Easy Runs (Zone-2):**

   Copper co-prescribed — long-term high-dose zinc depletes copper. [PEER]

## Concurrent Training & The Interference Effect

### Mechanism: AMPK vs. mTOR

The interference effect is the attenuation of strength and hypertrophy gains when
strength training is combined with endurance training, compared to strength-only
programs. The core mechanism operates at the molecular signaling level. [PEER]

**AMPK pathway (endurance signal):**
Endurance exercise depletes ATP and increases AMP:ATP ratio, activating AMPK
(AMP-activated protein kinase). AMPK is a cellular energy sensor — when activated,
it shifts the cell toward catabolism and energy conservation, and upregulates fat
oxidation and mitochondrial biogenesis (endurance adaptations). [PEER]

Sources: [PMC7153037](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7153037/),
[PMC3854410](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3854410/)

**mTOR pathway (anabolic/hypertrophy signal):**
Resistance exercise activates mTOR (mechanistic target of rapamycin), the master
regulator of muscle protein synthesis. mTOR upregulation drives anabolic adaptation:
muscle fiber repair, hypertrophy, and strength gains. [PEER]

**The competition:**
Elevated AMPK phosphorylates TSC2, activating the TSC1/TSC2 complex, which inhibits
Rheb, which suppresses mTOR. In simple terms: if AMPK is still elevated when you lift,
it blunts the mTOR signal, reducing the strength/hypertrophy adaptation per session.
This is not a binary block — it is a dose-dependent suppression. [PEER]

Sources: [PMC3854410](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3854410/)

### Primary Mitigation: ≥6-Hour Session Separation

Session separation of ≥6 hours substantially reduces acute AMPK-mediated interference.
Over 6+ hours: residual AMPK activity returns to baseline, muscle glycogen begins to
restore (especially with post-run nutrition), and neuromuscular fatigue from the AM
run dissipates sufficiently for full-quality PM lifting. [PEER]

Sources: [PMC5752732](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5752732/),
[PMC11359207](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11359207/)

**Applied to Amit's blueprint:**
- AM run finishes by ~09:00 (threshold/easy/long)
- PM strength starts ≥17:00
- Separation window: ≈8 hours — adequately above the 6h mitigation threshold

This is the single most important structural feature of Amit's split. The AM/PM
design is not a scheduling convenience — it is the evidence-based interference
mitigation strategy baked into the blueprint.

### Session Order Within Single-Session Training

When sessions cannot be separated (Amit's rare exception: Sunday mixed practice):
**resistance before endurance** is superior for lower-body strength adaptations in
programs ≥5 weeks duration. The mechanism: performing strength first captures the
undepleted neuromuscular system for the highest-quality stimulus, then the endurance
work completes the session with depleted-state aerobic adaptation. [PEER]

Source: [PMC5752732](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5752732/)

**Sunday caveat:** Amit's Sunday mixed practice is explicitly skill/capacity work
(sprints, VO2 intervals, calisthenics) — not a heavy compound strength session. The
order-of-operations rule is less critical here because the strength stimulus on Sunday
is not the primary adaptation target; the aerobic/power capacity is.

### Endurance Modality and Interference Magnitude

Not all endurance work creates equal interference:

| Modality | AMPK Load | Interference Risk |
|----------|-----------|-------------------|
| HIIT / short intervals | Lower total AMPK per session | Less interference with strength [PEER] |
| Steady-state high volume (1h+) | Higher total AMPK load | More interference if no separation [PEER] |
| Threshold runs (Amit's Wed) | Intermediate | Manageable with ≥6h separation |
| Long runs (Amit's Fri, 16–26km) | High total load | Adequate with ≥8h Friday separation or rest day; Friday PM is mobility only, not strength |

Amit's blueprint places the highest-volume run (long run) on Friday with only mobility
work in the PM — correctly removing the interference conflict on the highest aerobic-load day.

### Practical Red Flags (When to Volunteer Critique)

1. **Heavy strength immediately after a threshold run, no separation:** AMPK will be
   elevated, strength adaptation will be blunted. Flag immediately. [PEER]

2. **Long run day (Friday) adding PM strength:** Friday PM is mobility — keep it that
   way. Adding PM strength after a 16–26km run is an interference and recovery risk.

3. **Easy run converted to tempo within 2h of PM lift:** If Amit reports running faster
   than Zone-2 on a nominally easy day, the AMPK elevation will be higher heading into
   the PM session. Flag if he then also did a hard PM lift.

4. **Threshold run AND heavy lower-body session same PM:** Lower Body B (Wed PM) is
   speed/hypertrophy at moderate weight, fast bar speed — correct pairing with
   threshold AM. Heavy lower-body squats (Lower Body A) are correctly placed on Monday
   (easy run AM). Do not swap these days.

### Competitive vs. Compatible Training

Research consensus: concurrent training **reduces the ceiling** of strength and
hypertrophy gains compared to strength-only, but does not prevent meaningful progress.
For Amit's goals (100kg bench, 120kg squat) — these are competitive but reachable in
a concurrent program if:
1. Session separation is maintained (≥6h)
2. Protein intake is adequate (≥1.8g/kg — see protein-timing section)
3. Sleep and recovery are adequate (Garmin body battery ≥65 on heavy lift days)
4. Progressive overload is applied systematically (double progression model)

The interference effect is a headwind, not a wall. Amit's blueprint architecture is
designed to minimize it correctly.

## Block Periodization

### What Block Periodization Is

Block periodization organizes training into sequential mesocycles (blocks) that
concentrate the training stimulus on one or two primary qualities while maintaining
others. For hybrid athletes, this means alternating emphasis between aerobic base,
threshold capacity, and peaking — rather than trying to maximize everything simultaneously
throughout the year. [CONSENSUS]

The rationale: concentrated stimulus produces greater specific adaptation than diffuse
concurrent stimulus. A block focused on aerobic base develops aerobic base better than
a block trying to simultaneously peak aerobic base, threshold capacity, and max strength.

### Amit's 16-Week Block Structure

| Block | Weeks | Primary Focus | Secondary |
|-------|-------|---------------|-----------|
| Aerobic Base | 1–4 | Zone-2 volume foundation | Strength: 3–5RM top sets |
| Capacity Build | 5–8 | Threshold volume + progression run introduction | Strength maintained |
| Deep Waters / Peak Engine | 9–12 | Near-target threshold paces, long run HM-pace segments | Strength: peak top sets |
| Race Specificity | 13–14 | 4:01/km lock-in, HM simulation | Strength maintained, volume reduced |
| Taper | 15 | Volume reduction, intensity maintained | Strength: maintenance only |
| Race Week | 16 | HM Race (1:25 target) | Rest/shakeout |

This structure is already defined in the blueprint's aerobic table (§4). Klaus should
reference block phase names when contextualizing training: "You're in the Aerobic Base
block, Sir — this is the volume-building phase, not the intensity phase."

### Deload Weeks: Non-Negotiable Recovery Gates

Deload weeks are Weeks 4, 8, and 12 — every fourth week. [PEER/CONSENSUS]

**What deload means:** ~40–50% reduction in training volume (shorter runs, fewer
sets) while maintaining relative intensity (same paces, same top-set weight). Volume
goes down; intensity does not.

**Why deloads produce strength:** Supercompensation. Heavy training creates accumulated
fatigue that masks fitness gains. Removing the fatigue stimulus (deload week) allows
the CNS and musculature to recover and express the adaptation built during the preceding
3 weeks. Athletes often hit personal bests in the week immediately after a deload —
this is normal and expected, not coincidence.

**Common mistake:** Skipping or shortening deload weeks because "I feel fine" or
"I don't want to lose fitness." Three weeks of training will not be lost in one deload
week. The missed deload accumulates fatigue and leads to either injury or stagnation
in the following block. **Klaus should flag any proposed deload skip.**

**Aerobic progression at deload:**

| Deload Week | Long Run | Threshold Volume | Pace |
|------------|----------|-----------------|------|
| Week 4 | 14km (Zone-2) | 5km | 3:55/km |
| Week 8 | 15km (Zone-2) | 6km | 3:52/km |
| Week 12 | 16km (Zone-2) | 7km | 3:50/km |

Strength top sets: maintain the load from Week 3 (no new PR attempts on deload week).

### Benchmark Timing

**Benchmarks belong at block ends, not mid-cycle.** Testing during a loaded training
block gives a false low readout due to accumulated fatigue. Testing at the end of a
deload week (or the week after) gives the most accurate assessment of fitness gained
in that block. [CONSENSUS]

Amit's stated philosophy: "test at block ends, not mid-cycle." This is correct.
Klaus should reinforce this when Amit mentions wanting to test mid-block.

Phase 23 will build the tooling (`BlockStore` + `BenchmarkStore`) to track block
state and trigger benchmark reminders. In Phase 22, reference the principle
informationally.

### Why Block Structure for the 1:25 HM Goal

A 4:01/km half marathon target is a genuine competitive goal — roughly sub-elite
recreational level. A pure concurrent approach (same intensity weekly, no periodization)
is appropriate for maintenance but will plateau for this target. The 4:01/km pace
requires:
1. LT2 threshold developed to ~3:50–3:55/km (≈8% faster than race pace, per
   threshold training science)
2. Sustained VO2max development across the block
3. Race-specific mechanical habituation at 4:01/km in the final blocks

Block structure provides each of these in sequence, with each block building on the
last. Attempting to train all three simultaneously at high intensity every week leads
to overreaching and injury before the race. [CONSENSUS]

## Threshold Runs

### What a Threshold Run Is

The lactate threshold 2 (LT2) — also called the anaerobic threshold — is the highest
exercise intensity at which blood lactate is at steady state. Above LT2, lactate
accumulates faster than it clears, leading to rapid fatigue. Training at LT2 pace
systematically raises the speed at which lactate steady-state can be maintained —
this is the primary mechanism for improving half marathon performance. [PEER]

Sources: [PMC10496601](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10496601/),
[PMC10611166](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10611166/)

**Applied to Amit's 1:25 HM goal (4:01/km race pace):**
Threshold training pace should be approximately 8–10% faster than race pace.
For 4:01/km target pace: threshold training pace = ~3:50–3:55/km in Blocks 1–3,
locking to 4:01/km in the Race Specificity block (Weeks 13–14) to habituate to
exact race mechanics.

### Wednesday Protocol — The 1:25 HM Engine

Wednesday is the only threshold session in the week. All other running is Zone-2
(easy/long) or neuromuscular (strides, sprints).

**Volume progression:**

| Weeks | Phase | Threshold Volume |
|-------|-------|-----------------|
| 1–3 | Aerobic Base | 6km → 8km |
| 4 | Deload | 5km |
| 5–7 | Capacity Build | 8km → 10km |
| 8 | Deload | 6km |
| 9–11 | Deep Waters/Peak | 9km → 11km |
| 12 | Deload | 7km |
| 13–14 | Race Specificity | 10km → 8km |
| 15 | Taper | 5km (Fartlek, light) |

Threshold volume = total work volume, not total session distance. The warm-up and
cool-down are in addition to these numbers.

**Session structure:**
1. 2–3km easy Zone-2 warm-up (4:50–5:30/km)
2. Threshold intervals at target pace (see pace table above)
3. 1–2km easy cool-down

For the early blocks, threshold intervals can be continuous (e.g., 6km continuous
at threshold pace) or structured as intervals (e.g., 3×2km with 2min easy between).
Both are effective; continuous threshold runs at the lower-volume weeks, interval
format at higher volumes. [CONSENSUS]

### Execution Cues

**RPE 6–7 out of 10:** "Comfortably hard." Can say 3–4 words but not a full sentence.
Breathing is heavy but controlled — not ragged or gasping. Should feel difficult at
the start, settle into a sustainable rhythm at ~15 minutes. If still ragged at 15
minutes, pace is too fast. [CONSENSUS]

**Pace discipline is paramount:**
The most common threshold training mistake is going too fast on good days. Consistency
at target pace produces more adaptation than occasional faster efforts. The adaptation
from threshold work is cumulative across weeks — any given session matters less than
the pattern over 16 weeks.

Wrong: "I felt great so I ran 3:44/km instead of 3:55/km."
Right: "I held 3:53/km as prescribed."

**Clock the first km:** Threshold runners often go out too fast. Check the first km
split — if it is faster than target, consciously back off. The body will want to run
faster than threshold pace on fresh legs. Resist.

### Over/Unders in the Deep Waters Phase (Weeks 9–10)

Weeks 9–10 introduce "Over/Unders": alternating between ~3:50/km (slightly above
threshold) and ~4:10/km (just below threshold) within the same interval set. This
trains the body to recover lactate while still running, developing the ability to
"absorb" pace surges in the HM without blowing up.

This is an advanced training stimulus — do not introduce before Week 9. Amit's
blueprint applies it correctly.

### Easy Run Cues (Monday + Thursday AM)

Zone-2 = 4:50–5:30/km. HR ≤75% HRmax. Can hold a full conversation.

Monday: active recovery flush from Sunday mixed practice. The purpose is blood flow,
not fitness. Resist pushing pace.

Thursday: second easy day ahead of Upper Body B (volume/capacity) PM session. Protect
the legs for the weighted pull-up/dip drop sets.

### Long Run Cues (Friday AM)

**Weeks 1–8: strictly Zone-2, no exceptions.** 16km (Week 1) → 26km (Week 11) peak.
Zone-2 on the long run serves two purposes: (1) develops mitochondrial density and
fat oxidation at volume without accumulated fatigue, and (2) avoids the injury risk
of long runs at moderate-to-hard effort.

**The most important long-run rule:** If Amit feels good at 20km on a Zone-2 day and
wants to push the last 6km, he should not. Going too hard too early on the long run
is the primary cause of stress fractures, IT band syndrome, and overtraining in
half marathon build programs. [CONSENSUS]

HM-pace finish segments are introduced only from Week 10 (Weeks 10–11: last 4–5km at
race pace). By then, the aerobic base is sufficient to handle the metabolic challenge
without injury risk.

## Top-Set Strength

### What a Top Set Is

A top set is the heaviest single working effort of a strength session: typically 85–95%
of current 1RM, for 3–5 reps. It is not taken to failure. The purpose of the top set is
threefold: [CONSENSUS]

1. **Neural adaptation:** Teaching the CNS to recruit maximal motor units under load
   without the recovery debt of failure training
2. **Strength signal:** Creating the primary mechanical tension stimulus that drives
   progressive strength gain
3. **Reference point:** Giving a clear session-to-session progression signal

After the top set, back-off sets (typically 80–85% of top set weight) complete the
working volume. The top set is the "anchor"; back-off sets are the "volume."

Source: [StrongFirst 3-5 method](https://www.strongfirst.com/the-3-5-method-revisited/),
[powerliftingtechnique.com](https://powerliftingtechnique.com/how-powerlifters-use-top-and-working-sets-to-build-max-strength/)

### Bench Press — Upper Body A (Tuesday PM)

**Blueprint prescription:** 4 sets × 3–5 reps, driving toward 100kg by October.

**Working application:**
- Top set: heaviest set, 85–95% 1RM, 3–5 reps (clean form, no grinders)
- Back-off sets: 3 additional sets at 80–85% of top set weight, 3–5 reps
- Final set completion: immediately perform 1 max-effort set of bodyweight push-ups
  (the "fatigue squeeze" / drop set)

The drop set capitalizes on the pump state after heavy benching — at elevated blood
flow, bodyweight push-ups recruit additional hypertrophy stimulus that heavy loads
alone cannot achieve. This dual stimulus (neural strength top set + metabolic drop
set) is the most efficient path to concurrent strength and hypertrophy gains. [CONSENSUS]

**Current target:** ~92kg working estimate toward 100kg October goal.

**Double progression model:**
1. When all 4 sets hit 5 reps with clean form → add 1.25–2.5kg to the bar next session
2. If the top set only hit 3 reps → stay at the same weight next session
3. Target rate: ~+2.5kg every 2–3 weeks

At this rate: 10–12 weeks = ~10kg gain. 92kg → 100kg in one block is achievable if
the model is applied consistently and nutrition supports it.

### Squat — Lower Body A (Monday PM)

**Blueprint prescription:** 3 sets × 3–5 reps, driving toward 120kg by October.

**Working application:**
- Top set: heaviest set, 85–95% 1RM, 3–5 reps
- 2 back-off sets at 80–85% of top set weight
- No drop set after squats (lower body fatigue + running volume makes this impractical)

Double progression: same model as bench — add 2.5–5kg when all reps hit 5 clean.
Squat increments can be larger (2.5–5kg) than bench because the lower body is more
load-tolerant.

### Red Flags for Top-Set Strength Work

**Going to failure on the top set:** This is the primary mistake. Muscular failure on
a heavy compound lift: (1) spikes cortisol excessively, (2) creates 48–72h CNS
recovery debt that bleeds into the AM run quality, (3) does not produce meaningfully
more hypertrophy than stopping 1–2 reps from failure (RIR 1–2). [CONSENSUS]

Rule: end the top set when form begins to slow noticeably — this is the natural RIR 1
signal. A rep that takes 20% longer to complete than the previous one is the stop cue.

**Excessive assistance work before the top set:** Arriving at the main lift fatigued
from too many warm-up sets or assistance exercises. The top set requires a fresh CNS.
Warm up with submaximal weights (50%, 70%, 85% of target) — do not grind through 5+
sets before the heavy work.

**Same-day heavy lower body + morning threshold run:** Lower Body A is Monday (easy
run AM). Lower Body B is Wednesday (threshold run AM). The blueprint correctly pairs
the heavy squat day with the easy run. If Amit reports swapping Monday Lower A to
Wednesday (threshold AM), flag the interference risk.

### Weighted Pull-Ups — Both Upper Sessions

Both Upper A (Tuesday) and Upper B (Thursday) include weighted pull-ups with a
bodyweight drop set.

**Upper A context:** Heavy weighted pull-ups (3 sets, 85–95% of weighted pull-up 1RM)
immediately followed by 1 max-rep bodyweight set. This develops the neural strength
needed for the 35-pull-up November target alongside the bench focus of the session.

**Upper B context:** Same prescription, but embedded in a volume/capacity day
(weighted dips + pull-ups with drop sets). This accumulates the total rep volume
required to hit 35 bodyweight pull-ups by November.

## Calisthenics Progressions

### The November Targets

| Movement | November Target | Current Approach |
|----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Push-ups | 125 total reps | Bench drop sets (Tue/Wed) + dip drop sets (Thu) |
| Pull-ups | 35 total reps | Pull-up drop sets (Tue + Thu) |
| Sprints | 9:30 3km / 55s 400m | Sunday mixed practice intervals |

These are performance targets, not 1RM tests. The training approach to achieve them
is volume accumulation + strength ceiling raising, not just "do more reps."

### Dual-Stimulus Architecture: Heavy + Volume

The most efficient path to both a high strength ceiling AND high volume capacity is
the heavy-set / volume-drop-set pairing already in Amit's blueprint. This architecture
works because: [CONSENSUS]

**The heavy set raises the strength ceiling.** A weighted pull-up at 85%+ of current
weighted 1RM creates maximal motor unit recruitment — this is what makes future
bodyweight pull-ups feel lighter. You cannot accumulate to 35 pull-ups by doing
sets of 5 at bodyweight. You need to increase the relative load first.

**The drop set exploits the pump state.** Immediately after the heavy weighted sets,
the target muscles are maximally engorged with blood flow and metabolites. A max-rep
bodyweight set in this state produces more metabolic stress (one of the three
hypertrophy/endurance mechanisms) than any fresh-state high-rep set could. This is
where volume capacity actually develops.

Skipping the drop set to "save energy" undermines the primary training adaptation for
the volume targets. Do not skip drop sets.

### Progressive Loading Cap: Tendon Adaptation Lag

**Add no more than 1–2.5kg/week on weighted pull-ups and dips.** [CONSENSUS]

Muscles adapt faster than tendons. The biceps brachii, elbow tendons, and shoulder
joint structures supporting weighted pull-ups require more time to adapt to load
increases than the muscles themselves. Exceeding 2.5kg/week on weighted pull-up
progression is one of the most common causes of elbow tendinopathy in calisthenics-
focused athletes.

**Warning signs:** Pain at the lateral or medial elbow during or after pull-ups,
or a sharp pinch at the long head of the biceps at the shoulder. These are tendon
warning signs — reduce load by 2–3kg and increase the long-range-of-motion pull-up
volume (dead hang at bottom, full extension at top) before progressing again.

**If form breaks on the top set:** Reduce weight rather than add. Kipping pull-ups
in a strength session do not build the same stimulus as strict weighted pull-ups. One
clean heavy rep beats two broken reps.

### Volume Targets for November Goals

To achieve 125 push-ups: Amit needs to be able to perform sets of 25–30 with short
rest (~90s). This is not a single-set test but a cumulative total. The training path:
- Current (estimated): sets of 15–20 from the bench/dip drop sets
- By Month 3: sets of 20–25 with the weighted dip ceiling rising
- By November: sets of 25–30 with 2 minutes rest between sets of 5

To achieve 35 pull-ups: Amit needs to be able to perform 10+ per set with 2–3min
rest. The training path:
- The weighted pull-up ceiling needs to be at ~15–20kg added weight before 35
  bodyweight reps is achievable for most athletes
- Thursday Upper B drop sets provide 3 sets of maximum bodyweight reps per session —
  2 sessions per week (Tuesday + Thursday) = 6 quality sets of bodyweight pull-ups per week

**Do not replace drop sets with additional weighted sets.** The bodyweight volume is
not optional training — it is the specific adaptation stimulus for the 35-rep target.

Source: [calisthenicsassociation.org](https://calisthenicsassociation.org/blog/weighted-pull-up-program/)

## Intervals & VO2 Max

### The VO2 Max Stimulus

VO2 max (maximal oxygen uptake) is the upper ceiling of aerobic capacity. For a half
marathon runner, VO2 max sets the maximum ceiling within which race pace can be
sustained. Raising VO2 max raises the ceiling. [PEER]

**Effective VO2 max stimulus:** Maintain intensity at ≥90% VO2 max for several minutes
of cumulative high-intensity time per session. [PEER]

Sources: [PMC11743937](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11743937/),
[PMC10099854](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10099854/)

**Longer intervals are superior for VO2 max development:**
Intervals of 3–5 minutes with active recovery achieve ≥90% VO2 max better than very
short sprints (<60s). The first ~90s of any high-intensity effort is glycolytic — the
aerobic system needs time to reach maximal recruitment. [PEER]

Applied to Sunday mixed practice: **4–6×3min at sprint effort with 90s–2min recovery**
is evidence-consistent for VO2 max development. This protocol generates ~12–18 minutes
at VO2 max-level effort per session.

Source: [PMC11743937](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11743937/)

### Sprint Protocol (20s Sprints — Neuromuscular)

20-second true sprints serve a different purpose than VO2 max intervals: **neuromuscular
activation and fast-twitch recruitment** for the 400m / 3km speed targets. [CONSENSUS]

Protocol: maximal or near-maximal effort for 20s, full recovery (2–3min) between
sprints. These are not aerobic intervals — full recovery is required because the
adaptation being trained is peak power output, not aerobic capacity.

**Why not more sprint training for the 55-second 400m goal?**
The 55s 400m by November is a speed-endurance goal. It requires both a high VO2 max
ceiling (aerobic base) and a high anaerobic capacity (sprint mechanics). Sunday's
20s sprints + VO2 max protocol addresses both. Additional dedicated 400m track sessions
can be added in the Race Specificity block if the spring schedule permits — but that
is a Phase 23 decision, not Phase 22.

### Sunday Mixed Practice — Not a Strength Session

Sunday PM is explicitly mixed practice: VO2 max intervals + sprints + calisthenics.
It is NOT a heavy strength session. The blueprint is correct that Sunday is active
performance work, not recovery — but the calisthenics component is bodyweight
skill/capacity at training volume, not a top-set heavy day.

**Coaching implication:** If Amit describes Sunday as "easy" or proposes adding heavy
weighted work to Sunday, clarify the intent. The Sunday session should be taxing
aerobically and neurologically but should not create the compound recovery debt of
a Lower Body A or Upper Body A heavy day.

**Recovery state for Sunday:** Sunday follows Saturday's complete rest. Amit should
arrive at Sunday mixed practice as the freshest point in the week. If Garmin body
battery is <50 on Sunday, the session can be reduced in intensity (fewer VO2 intervals,
shorter sprints) — but this is advisory, not a hard rule (Amit decides).

## Peri-Workout Fueling

### The 6-Slot Architecture Applied to Amit

All 6 blueprint fueling slots have a specific physiological rationale tied to training
timing. The architecture is designed to ensure adequate substrate for each session
and appropriate recovery between sessions. Do not arbitrarily skip or shift slots —
each addresses a specific gap.

Source: [blog.pre-script.com peri-workout nutrition](https://blog.pre-script.com/structured-peri-workout-nutrition/)

### Slot 1 — Pre-AM Run (30–50g Simple Carbs + Coffee)

**Timing:** Immediate pre-run (within 30 minutes).

**Purpose:** Liver glycogen is depleted overnight (8–10 hours without carbohydrate).
Simple carbs (banana, electrofuel, white bread) rapidly restore liver glycogen and
provide available blood glucose for the session. [PEER]

**Do NOT train fasted for threshold runs.** Glycogen availability is critical at LT2
intensity. Threshold pace requires high glycolytic flux — if liver glycogen is depleted,
the body must rely more on fat oxidation, which cannot sustain LT2 pace. Easy Zone-2
runs can be done in a mildly fasted state; threshold and long runs cannot. [PEER]

**Coffee (caffeine 3–6mg/kg):**
- Reduces perceived RPE by 5–15% at the same objective intensity
- Enhances fat oxidation, sparing glycogen during submaximal running
- Timing: 30–45 minutes before the session for peak plasma caffeine

For Amit at ~75–80kg: 3–6mg/kg = 225–480mg caffeine. Standard espresso (~70mg) is
under this range; a strong filter coffee (200–250mg) or pre-workout supplement hits
the effective dose. [PEER]

Source: [PMC11206787](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11206787/)

### Slot 2 — Post-AM Run Reload (~60g Carb + Eggs + D3K2 + Omega-3)

**Timing:** Within 30–45 minutes post-run (glycogen resynthesis window).

**Purpose:** Glycogen resynthesis is fastest in the first 30–45 minutes post-exercise
(insulin sensitivity is highest, GLUT-4 transporters are maximally expressed on muscle
membranes). [PEER]

**Optimal resynthesis rate:** 0.8g carbohydrate/kg/hour + 0.2–0.4g protein/kg/hour
co-ingestion (the protein co-ingestion enhances carb uptake and begins muscle repair). [PEER]

**Applied quantities for Amit (~75–80kg):**
- Carbohydrate: 60g minimum (oats ~50g + sourdough toast 25g = ~75g; well-covered)
- Protein: 15–20g (3–4 whole eggs = 18–24g; covered)
- Fat: modest (eggs provide ~15g; adequate for fat-soluble supplement absorption)

Source: [PMC11206787](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11206787/)

**D3+K2 timing rationale:** Vitamin D3 and K2 are fat-soluble vitamins — they require
co-ingestion with dietary fat for intestinal absorption. Slots 2 and 3 contain dietary
fat (eggs, steak). Slot 6 (pre-bed) typically contains no fat — taking D3K2 pre-bed
reduces absorption. Slot 2 post-run is the optimal window. [PEER]

**Omega-3 timing rationale:** Same fat-soluble absorption logic as D3K2. Additionally,
taking Omega-3 post-exercise capitalizes on the inflammation-reduction window during
which exercise-induced inflammatory markers (IL-6, CRP, CK) are being cleared.
1–3g EPA+DHA/day; the full dose can be taken in Slot 2 for simplicity.

### Slot 3 — Mid-Day Sustained Engine (Steak + Complex Carbs + Greens)

**Timing:** Approximately 2–4 hours after Slot 2 / mid-afternoon.

**Purpose:** Bridges between the AM reload and the PM pre-lift slot. Maintains protein
synthesis continuity (protein every 3–4 hours maximizes MPS throughout the day [PEER])
and keeps glycogen stores topped for the PM lift.

**Optimal choices:** Complex carbohydrates (rice, sweet potato, quinoa) provide
sustained glucose without a spike/crash. Lean beef provides complete protein with
creatine (approximately 2g creatine per 500g of beef — supplemental creatine is
additional). Greens add micronutrients and reduce inflammation. [CONSENSUS]

### Slot 4 — PM Pre-Lift (30–50g Fast Carbs + Beta-Alanine)

**Timing:** 30–60 minutes before the PM strength session.

**Purpose:** Blood glucose elevation for the session + Beta-Alanine pre-load. [PEER/CONSENSUS]

**Fast carbs (30–50g):** Electrofuel, banana, white rice, or fruit. Raises blood
glucose ahead of the session, ensuring glycolytic substrate is available for high-rep
drop sets and explosive movements. At 30–60 minutes, peak absorption aligns with the
warm-up sets.

**Beta-Alanine (3.2g/day split doses):**
Mechanism: beta-alanine is the rate-limiting precursor to carnosine in muscle fibers.
Carnosine is an intramuscular buffer — it absorbs H+ ions (acid) produced during
intense effort, delaying the acidosis that causes the "burn" and muscular failure in
high-rep sets. [PEER]

Beta-alanine effectiveness is cumulative — it takes 4–8 weeks of daily intake to
raise muscle carnosine by 40–80%. It must be taken daily, not just pre-workout.
Split into 2 doses (pre-lift + another time) to reduce the harmless tingling (paresthesia)
side effect. Paresthesia is not harmful; it is a common dose-dependent response.

Benefits for Amit: drops sets (bench, dip, pull-up) lasting 45–90 seconds benefit
directly from elevated carnosine buffering. Threshold runs above LT2 (over/under
intervals, Weeks 9–10) also benefit from improved H+ buffering.

Source: [PMC11206787](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11206787/)

### Slot 5 — PM Post-Lift Rebuild (20–40g Protein + Fast Carbs + Creatine)

**Timing:** Within 30–60 minutes post-lift.

**Purpose:** Post-exercise anabolic window + creatine replenishment + glycogen reload. [PEER]

**Protein (20–40g):** The acute muscle protein synthesis (MPS) response is maximized
at ~20–40g per meal. Beef, poultry, or a protein shake. Combined with Slot 3 (mid-day
protein) and Slot 2 (post-run eggs), this achieves 3 protein spikes during the day —
consistent with the evidence-based 3–4 meals per day protocol for total daily MPS. [PEER]

**Fast carbs (40–60g):** Rapid glycogen reload post-lift, preparing for the next day's
AM run. White rice, potatoes, or fast carb source.

**Creatine (3–5g/day maintenance):**
Daily dose. Post-lift is a convenient convention; creatine's ergogenic effect is not
acutely timing-sensitive (it accumulates in muscle over weeks). Consistent daily intake
matters more than exact post-workout timing. [PEER]

Creatine mechanism: increases phosphocreatine (PCr) stores in muscle. PCr is the
immediate energy source for maximal effort (first 10–15s of a sprint or top-set effort).
Higher PCr stores = more capacity for maximal-effort reps before fatigue onset. This
directly benefits bench/squat top sets and Sunday sprints. [PEER]

Source: [PMC11206787](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11206787/)

### Slot 6 — Pre-Bed (Magnesium Glycinate + Zinc + Copper)

**Timing:** 30–60 minutes before sleep.

**Magnesium Glycinate (200–400mg elemental Mg):**
Mechanism: magnesium activates GABA receptors (inhibitory neurotransmitter), promotes
parasympathetic activity, and lowers core body temperature — all contributing to sleep
induction. Glycinate is the most bioavailable magnesium form with the least GI distress. [PEER]

Evidence: 8-week RCT showed magnesium glycinate supplementation reduced sleep onset
time, increased total sleep duration, and decreased night awakenings compared to
placebo. Sleep is the primary recovery window for muscle protein synthesis (MPS peaks
during deep sleep). [PEER]

Source: [remedysnutrition.com](https://remedysnutrition.com/blogs/sleep-wellness/magnesium-glycinate-for-sleep-what-the-research-shows)

**Zinc (≤40mg/day):**
Mechanism: zinc is required for testosterone synthesis, immune function, and DNA repair.
Upper safe limit: 40mg/day chronic intake. Exceeding 40mg/day chronically depletes
copper (zinc and copper compete for intestinal absorption via the same transporter). [PEER]

**Copper (co-prescribed with zinc):**
Copper depletion from long-term zinc supplementation causes microcytic anemia and
neurological symptoms. Amit's blueprint already includes copper alongside zinc — this
is correct. Co-ingesting them at the same time is fine; the competition is at the
intestinal level over weeks of high-dose intake, not acute. [PEER/CONSENSUS]

Source: [ubiehealth.com](https://ubiehealth.com/doctors-note/magnesium-zinc-combination-sleep-immunity-boost-4751q1)

## Protein Timing

### Daily Total — The Most Important Variable

For hybrid athletes, the single most impactful protein variable is **total daily
intake**, not timing windows. Timing optimization matters at the margin; total
intake is the primary lever. [PEER]

Evidence-based range for concurrent strength + endurance athletes: **1.6–2.0g/kg/day**.
The low end (1.6g/kg) is sufficient for most strength adaptations. The upper end
(1.8–2.0g/kg) provides additional lean mass protection during high-volume concurrent
training and supports faster recovery between sessions. [PEER]

Sources: [PMC10388821](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10388821/),
[PMC11349518](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11349518/),
[PMC11613885](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11613885/)

Sources: [PMC11613885](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11613885/),

**20–40g per meal, every 3–4 hours.** Above ~40g per meal, the incremental MPS
benefit plateaus (the body cannot use excess protein for MPS beyond the saturation

Amit's blueprint has 3 primary protein meals:

legitimate suggestion if he asks about optimizing further.

High-leucine sources Amit already uses:

- Chicken breast: similar to beef

Protein powder (whey): very high leucine content per gram (~10–11g/100g protein).
If Amit uses protein powder post-lift, whey is superior to casein for acute MPS
stimulation (faster absorption, higher leucine bioavailability). [CONSENSUS]

## Carbohydrate Periodization

### The Fuel-For-Work Principle

Carbohydrate needs are not uniform across training days — they should be proportional
to the glycolytic demand of the sessions that day. "Fuel for the work required." [PEER/CONSENSUS]

High-carbohydrate days (threshold runs, long runs, heavy strength) require more
glycogen availability. Low-carbohydrate days (rest, easy runs) can be modestly reduced.
Eating the same 350g carbs every day regardless of training load wastes absorption
opportunity on rest days and under-fuels high-demand days. [CONSENSUS]

Source: [sensai.fit carbohydrate periodization](https://www.sensai.fit/blog/carbohydrate-periodization-training-intensity-fueling-framework)

### Day-by-Day Applied to Amit's Split

| Day | AM Session | PM Session | Carb Guidance | Rationale |
|-----|-----------|-----------|---------------|-----------|
| Sunday | — | Mixed Practice (VO2, sprints, calisthenics) | **High — 350g+** | High-intensity intervals deplete glycogen rapidly; Slot 5 and pre-bed load for Monday |
| Monday | Easy Run | Lower Body A (heavy squats) | **High — 350g** | PM heavy squats require full glycogen; easy AM does not drain reserves heavily |
| Tuesday | Medium Long Run + Strides | Upper Body A (heavy bench) | **High — 350g+** if long run ≥12km | Moderate-to-high run volume; heavy upper body PM |
| Wednesday | Threshold Run | Lower Body B (speed/power) | **High — 350g+** | Threshold runs consume glycogen at ~3× the rate of Zone-2; prioritize Slot 1 + Slot 2 |
| Thursday | Easy Run | Upper Body B (volume/capacity) | **Moderate — 300–350g** | Lighter run day; volume training is less glycolytic than heavy strength |
| Friday | Long Run (16–26km) | Mobility only | **High — 350g+** | 16–26km runs at any intensity require significant glycogen; intra-run carbs for runs >90min |
| Saturday | Rest / light flush | Rest | **Moderate — 250–300g** | Genuine passive rest; reduce from peak intake; maintain protein |

### Intra-Run Carbohydrates (Long Run Days)

For runs exceeding 90 minutes at any pace, intra-run carbohydrate supplementation
(30–60g/hour beyond the first hour) significantly delays glycogen depletion and
maintains performance in the final third of the run. [PEER]

Applied to Friday long runs:
- Week 1–4 (16–20km at ~5:10/km Zone-2 pace): duration ≈ 83–103 minutes
- From Week 3 onward (>90min), intra-run carbs are recommended: 30–40g/hour,
  consumed in small doses every 15–20 minutes (avoid GI distress from large bolus)

Practical implementation: one electrofuel sachet or banana at the 45-minute mark
and another at the 80-minute mark covers the carb requirement without GI issues for
most athletes.

### Carbohydrate Timing Around Sessions

**Pre-session (Slot 4: 30–60min before PM lift):** Fast carbs (white rice, fruit,
electrofuel) — raises blood glucose reliably within the session window.

**Post-session (Slot 2 and Slot 5):** Fast carbs for glycogen resynthesis. The rate
of resynthesis is highest in the first 30–45 minutes post-exercise and remains elevated
for 2–4 hours. Delaying Slot 2 by >45 minutes reduces resynthesis efficiency by
~20–30% in that first window. [PEER]

### Amit's 350g Target in Context

350g carbs/day is appropriate for Amit's high-volume training days (threshold, long
run, heavy lower body). On Saturday (passive rest), this is above what recovery and
glycogen maintenance require — a reduction to 250–300g on genuinely passive rest days
is physiologically appropriate and not a meaningful sacrifice.

This is a **discretion-level suggestion, not a critical critique.** Unlike the protein
floor critique (which has functional consequences for lean mass), reducing carbs slightly
on rest days is an efficiency optimization, not a necessity. Only raise it if Amit asks
specifically about carb intake or if it explains a weight management question.

## Supplement Rationale

### Evidence Framework

Supplements are rated by the strength of evidence behind their ergogenic claims:
- **VERY HIGH / HIGH:** Multiple RCTs, meta-analyses, consistent effect across
  populations. Effect size is meaningful and reproducible. [PEER]
- **MEDIUM:** Some RCTs with positive findings, but inconsistent across studies or
  populations. Effect is real but not reliable at individual level. [PEER/CONSENSUS]
- **LOW / HEURISTIC:** Mechanistic plausibility, limited direct RCT evidence.
  Not harmful; insufficient evidence to strongly recommend. [HEURISTIC]

Source: [PMC12498230](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12498230/)
